GCLC Acts Summarized

How To Use

The short title and section number are directly from the act PDFs. You can read these one by one, try to understand what each act means, and try to remember which short title relates to which concept, so in the exam, you can CTRL+F the short title and refer to that. Not all sections are covered in this; only the ones that are important were included.

Computer Crime Act

Section Number Short Title Description Example Offence
3 Securing unauthorised access to a computer an offence Anyone who intentionally accesses a computer or information without lawful authority is liable to a fine up to Rs. 100,000 or imprisonment up to 5 years or both. Hacking into someone's email account without permission.
4 Doing any act to secure unauthorised access in order to commit an offence Anyone who intentionally accesses a computer or information without lawful authority with the intent to commit another offence is liable to a fine up to Rs. 200,000 or imprisonment up to 5 years or both. Hacking into a bank's system to steal money.
5 Causing a computer to perform a function without lawful authority an offence Anyone who intentionally causes a computer to perform a function knowing it will result in unauthorised modification or damage is liable to a fine up to Rs. 300,000 or imprisonment up to 5 years or both. Spreading malware that deletes files on other computers.
6 Offences committed against national security, etc. Anyone who causes a computer to perform a function knowing it poses a danger to national security, the economy, or public order is punishable by imprisonment up to 5 years. Launching a cyber-attack on government infrastructure.
7 Dealing with data, etc., unlawfully obtained an offence Anyone who deals with information obtained unlawfully from a computer, knowing it was obtained without authority, is liable to a fine between Rs. 100,000 and Rs. 300,000 or imprisonment between 6 months and 3 years or both. Buying stolen personal data from a hacker.
8 Illegal interception of data an offence Anyone who intercepts data or communications from or within a computer without lawful authority is liable to a fine between Rs. 100,000 and Rs. 300,000 or imprisonment between 6 months and 3 years or both. Eavesdropping on someoneâ€™s online conversations without consent.
9 Using of illegal devices an offence Anyone who produces, sells, or distributes devices or information designed to access computers unlawfully, intending them to be used for an offence, is liable to a fine between Rs. 100,000 and Rs. 300,000 or imprisonment between 6 months and 3 years or both. Selling software that cracks passwords to access secured systems.
10 Unauthorised disclosure of information enabling access to a service, an offence Anyone entrusted with access information who discloses it without authority or breaches a contract is liable to a fine between Rs. 100,000 and Rs. 300,000 or imprisonment between 6 months and 3 years or both. An employee sharing company login details with outsiders without permission.
11 Attempts to commit offence Anyone who attempts to commit or cause an offence under sections 3 to 14 is liable to half the maximum fine or imprisonment prescribed for that offence or both. Trying but failing to hack into a secure network.
12 Abetment of an offence Anyone who abets (aids or encourages) the commission of an offence is liable to the same punishment as for the offence itself if committed, or lesser punishment if not committed. Assisting someone by providing tools to commit a cybercrime.
13 Conspiring to commit an offence Anyone who conspires with others to commit an offence is liable to the punishment prescribed for abetment of that offence. Planning with others to launch a phishing attack on an organization.
14 Compensation to be awarded for loss or damage consequent to an offence A victim who suffers loss or damage will be paid money A company that loses money due to a cyber-attack can claim compensation.
16 Offence under the Act to be cognizable offence. All offences under this Act are considered cognizable offences, meaning police can arrest without a warrant. Police arrest a suspect for hacking without needing a warrant due to the urgency of the offence.
17 Appointment of a Panel of Experts The Minister may appoint qualified experts in electronic engineering or software technology to assist police officers in investigations of offences under this Act. A cybersecurity expert is appointed to help the police investigate a complex hacking incident.
18 Powers of Search and Seizure with Warrant Experts or police officers can obtain information or intercept communications with a Magistrate's warrant; in urgent cases, they can act without a warrant under certain conditions. Police obtain a warrant to access email records of a suspect; in an urgent case, they intercept data immediately to prevent loss of evidence.
19 Preservation of Information Experts or police can require a person to preserve data for up to 7 days, extendable to 90 days by a Magistrate, to prevent loss or alteration during an investigation. A service provider is instructed to preserve server logs relevant to an ongoing cybercrime investigation.
20 Normal Use of Computer Not to be Hampered Investigators must ensure that legitimate business operations are not hindered during a search or seizure unless absolutely necessary. During a search of a company's computer system, investigators avoid disrupting daily operations unless critical for the investigation.
21 Power of Police Officer to Arrest, Search, and Seize Police officers can arrest, search, and seize information during an investigation, but must produce the suspect before a Magistrate within 24 hours. Only certified officers with adequate ICT expertise can access computers. A certified cybercrime officer arrests a suspect and seizes their laptop, presenting them to court within the stipulated time frame.
22 Police Officer to Record and Afford Access to Seized Data Police must provide a list of seized items and, upon request, allow the owner to copy data unless it would prejudice the investigation. After seizing a computer, police give the owner a list of seized items and allow data copying that doesn't compromise the case.
23 Duty to Assist Investigation Individuals must comply with requests for assistance or disclosure during an investigation; failure to comply or obstruction is an offence punishable by fine or imprisonment. A network administrator refuses to provide access logs to investigators and is charged for obstructing the investigation.
24 Confidentiality of information obtained in the course of an investigation Investigators and service providers must keep information obtained during investigations confidential; unauthorized disclosure is an offence. An investigator leaks details of an ongoing cybercrime case to the media and faces legal consequences for breaching confidentiality.
25 Jurisdiction The High Court has jurisdiction over offences under this Act; for extradition cases, the High Court in Colombo has exclusive jurisdiction. A cybercrime case involving international elements is tried in the High Court of Colombo due to extradition considerations.
26 Proof of Documents Issued by An Expert or a Police Officer Documents issued by experts or police officers and authenticated are admissible as evidence and are prima facie proof of the facts stated. An expert's certified report on digital evidence is accepted in court without requiring further validation.
27 Amendment of the Schedule to the Extradition Law, No. 8 of 1977 Adds offences under this Act to the list of extraditable offences in the Extradition Law, No. 8 of 1977. A suspect who committed cybercrimes in Sri Lanka and fled abroad can be extradited back to Sri Lanka for trial.
28 Immunity from Legal Proceedings Experts and police officers acting in good faith under this Act are protected from civil or criminal liability for their lawful actions. An investigator is protected from a lawsuit when acting within the scope of their duties during a cybercrime investigation.

Personal Data Protection Act

Section Number Short Title Description Example Scenario
4 Compliance with the Data Protection Obligations Controllers must process personal data in compliance with the obligations specified in the Act. A company reviews its data processing activities to ensure they meet all requirements of the Personal Data Protection Act.
5 Obligation to Process Personal Data in a Lawful Manner Personal data processing is lawful if it meets conditions specified in Schedules I to IV, such as obtaining consent or fulfilling legal obligations. A business obtains explicit consent from customers before collecting their personal information for marketing purposes.
6 Obligation to Define a Purpose for Personal Data Processing Controllers must process personal data for specific, explicit, and legitimate purposes and not use it in ways incompatible with those purposes. An app collects user data solely to provide personalized services and doesn't share it with third parties for unrelated purposes.
7 Obligation to Confine Personal Data Processing to the Defined Purpose Personal data collected must be adequate, relevant, and proportionate to the purpose for which it is processed. A retailer collects only necessary customer details required to deliver products, not additional unnecessary personal information.
8 Obligation to Ensure Accuracy Controllers must ensure personal data is accurate and kept up to date, correcting any inaccuracies without undue delay. An employer updates an employee's contact information promptly after being notified of a change in address.
9 Obligation to Limit the Period of Retention Personal data should be kept only as long as necessary for the purposes it was collected, unless required longer for public interest archiving, research, or legal obligations. A service provider deletes former customers' personal data after a set period unless retention is required by law.
10 Obligation to Maintain Integrity and Confidentiality Controllers must use appropriate measures to protect personal data against unauthorized processing, loss, destruction, or damage, such as encryption or access controls. A bank implements robust cybersecurity measures to safeguard customer account information from breaches.
11 Obligation to Process Personal Data in a Transparent Manner Controllers must provide data subjects with specified information in a clear, transparent, and easily accessible form, including decisions made regarding their data requests. A website provides a user-friendly privacy policy and informs users about how their data is used and how to exercise their rights.
12 Accountability in the Processing of Personal Data Controllers must implement internal controls (Data Protection Management Programme) to demonstrate compliance with data protection obligations, including record-keeping and facilitating data subjects' rights. A company establishes policies and training programs to ensure employees handle personal data in compliance with the Act.
13 Right of Access to Personal Data Data subjects have the right to request access to their personal data and confirm whether it has been processed, receiving specified information upon written request. An individual requests a copy of their personal data held by a healthcare provider.
14 Right of withdrawal of the consent and the right to object to processing Data subjects can withdraw their consent at any time or object to processing based on certain lawful grounds, without affecting prior lawful processing. A user opts out of a newsletter by withdrawing consent, and the company stops sending emails but retains records of past communications as allowed.
15 Right to Rectification or Completion Data subjects can request correction or completion of inaccurate or incomplete personal data, and controllers must rectify it without undue delay. A customer notices their name is misspelled in a company's records and requests a correction, which the company promptly makes.
16 Right to Erasure Data subjects can request the deletion of their personal data under certain circumstances, such as unlawful processing or withdrawal of consent. A former employee asks their previous employer to delete personal data that's no longer necessary, and the employer complies unless retention is legally required.
17 Grant or refusal of rectification, completion, erasure or refrain from further processing Controllers must inform data subjects within 21 working days whether their requests under Sections 13-16 are granted or refused, providing reasons if refused and information on the right to appeal. An organization informs an individual that their request to delete data cannot be fulfilled due to ongoing legal obligations but provides information on how to appeal.
18 Automated individual decision making Data subjects can request a review of decisions made solely by automated processing that have significant impacts, except in certain authorized situations. A person denied a loan by an automated system requests a human review of the decision.
19 Right of appeal of the data subjects to the Authority and the process of determination of such appeal Data subjects can appeal to the Data Protection Authority if dissatisfied with a controller's response to their requests regarding their personal data. An individual appeals to the Authority after a company refuses to correct their personal data without a valid reason.
20 Designation or Appointment of the Data Protection Officer Certain controllers and processors must appoint a Data Protection Officer (DPO) to ensure compliance with the Act, especially in cases involving large-scale data processing or sensitive data. A large financial institution appoints a DPO to oversee data protection practices across the organization.
21 Additional obligations of a controller Controllers must use processors that comply with the Act, ensure processors are bound by contracts specifying processing details, and share responsibility if they jointly determine processing purposes. A company contracts a third-party data processor and includes detailed data protection clauses in the agreement.
22 Additional obligations of the processors Processors must process data only on the controller's instructions, ensure confidentiality, assist with compliance audits, and return or erase data after services are completed. An IT service provider processes data as per the client's instructions and deletes all personal data after the contract ends.
23 Personal Data Breach Notifications Controllers must notify the Authority of personal data breaches in a specified form and timeframe, with the Authority determining when data subjects should be notified and the details required. A company experiences a data breach and promptly informs the Authority, following guidelines on how to report the incident.
24 Personal Data Protection Impact Assessments Controllers must conduct impact assessments before processing that involves high risks, such as systematic profiling or large-scale processing of sensitive data, to evaluate and mitigate potential harm to data subjects. Before launching a new app that collects biometric data, a company performs a Data Protection Impact Assessment to identify and mitigate privacy risks.
25 Measures to Mitigate Risks of Harm and the Requirement for Prior Consultation If risks of harm remain after mitigation efforts, controllers must consult the Authority before processing; the Authority can issue instructions to mitigate risks or cease processing. A business consults the Authority when it cannot fully mitigate risks identified in an impact assessment for a new data analytics project.
26 Cross-Border Data Flow Regulates transferring personal data outside Sri Lanka, requiring adequacy decisions by the Minister or appropriate safeguards, with special restrictions for public authorities. An organization ensures compliance by obtaining explicit consent from data subjects or verifying that the recipient country has adequate data protection laws before transferring data abroad.
27 Solicited Messages to Data Subjects by Controllers Controllers can send messages (e.g., marketing communications) to data subjects only if they have consented, must provide opt-out options, and inform data subjects of the message's nature and sender's identity. A retailer sends promotional emails only to customers who have opted in and includes an unsubscribe link in every email.

Payment Devices Fraud Act

Section Number Short Title Description Example Scenario
3 Acts Amounting to Payment Devices Fraud Defines various fraudulent activities related to payment devices, such as possessing equipment for making counterfeit cards, tampering with devices, unauthorized use, producing or trafficking counterfeit cards, and other related offences. Someone skims card information at an ATM using a hidden device to create counterfeit cards; this is payment device fraud under the Act.
4 Attempts to Commit Offences States that attempting to commit any offence under the Act is itself an offence, punishable with fines or imprisonment up to half the maximum penalty for the completed offence. A person is caught trying to install a skimming device on a card reader but is stopped before completing the act; they can be charged with an attempt.
5 Abetment of an Offence Any person who assists or encourages another to commit an offence under the Act is guilty of abetment and faces penalties similar to those for the main offence if it occurs, or lesser penalties if it does not. A store employee provides customer card details to someone planning to commit fraud; they are guilty of abetment.
6 Conspiracy to Commit an Offence Conspiring with others to commit an offence under the Act is itself an offence, punishable with the same penalties as abetment. A group plans together to hack into a payment processing system; even if they don't carry it out, they can be charged with conspiracy.
7 Offences under this Act to be investigated under the provisions of the Code of Criminal Procedure Act Offences under this Act are to be investigated and tried according to the procedures in the Code of Criminal Procedure Act, No. 15 of 1979. Police follow standard legal procedures when investigating a payment device fraud case.
8 Experts and Their Powers and Functions The Minister can appoint experts in electronic or software engineering to assist police in investigations; experts have powers to enter premises, access systems, require information, and assist in gathering evidence. An IT expert is appointed to help police investigate a complex credit card fraud involving malware.
9 Legitimate use of network not to be hampered Investigators must avoid disrupting legitimate business operations of payment networks during investigations unless necessary to prevent offences or secure evidence. While examining a bank's payment system, investigators ensure normal transactions continue uninterrupted.
10 Powers of Police Officer to Arrest, Search, and Seize Police officers can arrest suspects and search or seize evidence according to the law; only qualified officers can access payment devices or systems during investigations. A trained officer with ICT expertise seizes counterfeit card-making equipment during a raid.
11 Duty to Assist Investigation Individuals must comply with requests from police or experts during investigations; obstructing or failing to assist is an offence punishable by fine or imprisonment. A shop owner refuses to provide CCTV footage during a fraud investigation and can be charged for non-compliance.
12 Confidentiality of information obtained in the course of an investigation Investigators and those involved must keep information obtained during investigations confidential; unauthorized disclosure is an offence. An employee shares details of an ongoing fraud investigation with outsiders and faces legal consequences for breaching confidentiality.
14 Offences by Bodies of Persons If a company or firm commits an offence under the Act, its directors, officers, or partners can be held liable unless they can prove lack of knowledge or due diligence to prevent the offence. The directors of a company that facilitates payment fraud through negligence can be prosecuted individually.
15 Court May Award Compensation Courts can direct that fines imposed be used to compensate issuers or victims who suffered loss and order the return of seized property to rightful owners. A fraudster is fined, and the court orders that the fine be paid to the bank that suffered losses from the fraud.
17 Presumptions Possession of counterfeit payment devices or equipment without authority is presumed to be for the purpose of committing fraud unless proven otherwise. A person found with multiple fake credit cards is presumed to intend to commit fraud unless they can prove a legitimate reason.
19 High Court to Have Jurisdiction The High Court of the Western Province holden in Colombo has jurisdiction over offences under this Act, regardless of where in Sri Lanka the offence occurred. A payment device fraud case from any part of the country is tried in the High Court in Colombo.
20 Offences under this Act to be cognizable offences Offences under this Act are cognizable, meaning police can arrest without a warrant. Police arrest a suspect involved in payment device fraud without needing a warrant due to the urgency of the crime.
21 Payment of Expenses Incurred on Action Expenses incurred by experts or police officers in good faith during investigations or legal proceedings are to be paid by the issuer or acquirer alleging the offence unless recovered in court. An expert's costs for assisting in a fraud investigation are covered by the bank that reported the fraud.
26 Right of persons arrested under this Act Non-citizens arrested under the Act have the right to communicate with their country's representatives and be visited by them; procedural lapses in requests do not invalidate legal proceedings if substantial compliance is met. A foreign national arrested for card fraud is allowed to contact their embassy and receive consular assistance.

Prevention of Money Laundering Act

Section Number Short Title Description Example Scenario
3 Offence of Money Laundering Defines money laundering as engaging in transactions involving property derived from unlawful activities, knowing or believing it to be so; sets penalties including fines, imprisonment, and forfeiture of assets. A person knowingly invests money obtained from drug trafficking into real estate; they can be charged with money laundering.
4 Presumption Establishes that property acquired by a person is presumed to be from unlawful activities if it cannot be explained as part of known income or receipts, until proven otherwise. An individual with no significant income purchases a luxury car; it's presumed the funds are from illegal activities unless they prove otherwise.
5 Duty of certain persons to disclose knowledge or belief of acts constituting the offence of money laundering Requires individuals who, in the course of their work, know or suspect property is from unlawful activity to report it to the Financial Intelligence Unit; failure to do so is an offence. An accountant notices suspicious transactions in a client's accounts suggesting money laundering and must report it to authorities.
6 Divulging etc. of information an offence Makes it an offence to disclose information about money laundering investigations that could prejudice the investigation, including revealing the identity of suspects or falsifying relevant documents. A bank employee leaks details of a money laundering investigation to the suspect, hindering the investigation; this is an offence under the Act.
7 Freezing of Property Allows senior police officers to issue a Freezing Order to prohibit transactions on accounts or properties suspected of being connected to money laundering for up to seven days. Police freeze a suspect's bank account to prevent funds from being moved while investigating money laundering activities.
9 Transactions in contravention of the Freezing Order to be null and void. States that no transactions can occur on frozen accounts or properties while a Freezing Order is in force; any transactions made are null and void. Any attempt by a suspect to withdraw money from a frozen account is invalidated and has no legal effect.
13 Forfeiture of property in relation to which offence of money laundering has been committed Upon conviction, courts can order the forfeiture of property derived from unlawful activities; considers the rights of bona fide purchasers; provides for presumption of illicit origin if property exceeds known income and cannot be explained. A convicted money launderer's unexplained wealth is seized by the state after the court orders forfeiture of assets.
18 Offences by Body of Persons Holds directors, officers, partners, or members of a company, firm, or unincorporated body liable if an offence is committed by that entity, unless they prove lack of knowledge or due diligence to prevent the offence. The directors of a company involved in laundering money can be prosecuted individually unless they can show they were unaware and took steps to prevent it.
19 Protection for action taken under this Act or on direction of Authority Provides immunity from lawsuits or prosecutions for the Financial Intelligence Unit, its agents, institutions, and their staff for actions done in good faith under the Act. An officer reporting suspicious transactions is protected from being sued by the client for breach of confidentiality.
20 Falsification of Documents Criminalizes the falsification, concealment, destruction, or disposal of documents relevant to the exercise of powers under the Act; sets penalties for such actions. Destroying records to hide evidence of money laundering during an investigation is an offence punishable under the Act.
21 Offences Makes it an offence to falsify, conceal, or destroy documents relevant to the execution of orders under the Act, or to divulge information about investigations that may prejudice them. A person tipped off suspects about upcoming searches, hindering law enforcement efforts; this is punishable under the Act.
28 Rights of Persons Arrested Under this Act Ensures that non-citizens arrested under the Act have the right to communicate with their country's representative; validates requests even if procedural requirements are not fully met if substantial compliance exists. A foreign national arrested for money laundering is allowed to contact their embassy for assistance.
34 Sinhala Text to Prevail in Case of Inconsistency Establishes that in the event of inconsistency between Sinhala and Tamil texts of the Act, the Sinhala text prevails. Legal interpretations will defer to the Sinhala version if there's a discrepancy between language versions of the Act.

Intellectual Property Act

Section Number Short Title Description Example Scenario
6 Works Protected Lists the types of original literary, artistic, and scientific works that are protected, including books, music, art, software, and more. A novelist writes a new book; their work is automatically protected under the Act.
7 Derivative Works Protects translations, adaptations, arrangements, and collections of works or data that are original due to selection or arrangement. A translator renders a novel into another language; the translation is protected as a derivative work.
8 Works Not Protected Specifies that ideas, procedures, methods, concepts, official texts, and news of the day are not protected by copyright. An idea for a new game show isn't protected until it's expressed as a script or format; others can use similar ideas.
9 Economic Rights Grants the copyright owner exclusive rights to reproduce, distribute, rent, import, display, perform, broadcast, and communicate the work to the public. A musician controls who can sell copies of their songs and can authorize others to perform their music publicly.
10 Moral Rights Gives authors the right to be identified as the creator and to object to derogatory treatment of their work, even after transferring economic rights. An artist objects to modifications of their painting that distort its meaning, protecting their reputation.
11 Fair Use Allows limited use of copyrighted works without permission for purposes like criticism, comment, news reporting, teaching, scholarship, or research. A teacher photocopies a few pages from a textbook for classroom discussion, which is permissible under fair use.
13 Duration of Copyright Specifies that copyright lasts for the author's lifetime plus 70 years, with variations for joint works, anonymous works, and works of applied art. A photographer's images are protected during their life and for 70 years after their death, preventing unauthorized use during that time.
16 Assignment or Licence of Author's Rights Allows copyright owners to license or transfer their economic rights to others through a written agreement. A writer signs a contract granting a publisher the rights to print and sell their book.
19 Equitable Remuneration for Use of Sound Recordings Requires users who broadcast or publicly perform commercially published sound recordings to pay equitable remuneration to performers and producers. A radio station plays songs and must pay royalties that are shared between the recording artists and producers.
21 Limitations on Protection Specifies that certain acts are not infringements, such as personal use, reporting news, teaching, or uses allowed under fair use provisions. An individual makes a personal copy of a purchased CD for private listening; this does not infringe copyright.
22 Enforcement of rights and disputes resolution Provides legal remedies for infringement, including injunctions, damages, and destruction of infringing copies; allows rights holders to seek court orders. A software company sues a business that is using pirated copies of its software, seeking damages and an order to destroy the illegal copies.
23 Measures, remedies and sanctions against abuses in respect of technical means Prohibits the manufacture or sale of devices designed to circumvent copy protection or encryption; treats such acts as infringements. Selling devices that disable DVD copy protection is illegal and considered an infringement under the Act.
24 Protection of Expressions of Folklore and Damages Protects expressions of folklore against misuse and requires authorization for commercial or public use; fees collected are used for cultural development. A company wants to use traditional folk songs in an advertisement and must obtain permission and pay a fee to the designated authority.
125 Form and Record of Licence Contract Requires that licence contracts be in writing and allows parties to record contract particulars with the Director-General if they wish. An artist licenses the use of their artwork to a merchandise company through a written agreement and records it with the relevant authority.
178 Infringement of Copyright Defines penalties for willful infringement of copyright, including fines and imprisonment; allows courts to order destruction of infringing copies. An individual selling pirated movies is prosecuted, fined, and may face imprisonment, with illegal copies confiscated and destroyed by court order.

Electronic Transactions Act

Section Number Short Title Description Example Scenario
3 Legal Recognition of Electronic Records Electronic records and communications cannot be denied legal recognition, validity, or enforceability solely because they are in electronic form. An agreement made via email is legally valid, just like a paper contract signed in person.
4 Requirement for Writing Where the law requires information to be in writing, that requirement is satisfied by an electronic record if the information is accessible for future use. A digital contract stored electronically meets the legal requirement for a written contract if it can be retrieved and read later.
5 Requirements for Original Form Information required to be presented or retained in its original form is satisfied by an electronic record if its integrity is assured and it is usable for future reference. A PDF invoice stored electronically is considered an original document if it hasn't been altered and can be accessed when needed.
6 Requirements for Retention Legal requirements to retain information are satisfied by retaining it electronically if it is accessible, accurately represents the original, and includes origin and timestamp information. A company keeps digital copies of invoices and emails, fulfilling legal record-keeping requirements.
7 Legal Recognition of Electronic Signatures Electronic signatures are legally recognized and satisfy the requirement of a signature under any law. Signing a contract online using an electronic signature is legally binding, just like a handwritten signature on paper.
8 Use of electronic records and electronic signatures in Government institutions and statutory bodies Government bodies can accept electronic records and signatures for filings, applications, licenses, payments, etc., as specified by relevant authorities. Applying for a government permit online using electronic forms and signing with an electronic signature.
9 Publication in electronic forms of Gazette etc. deemed to be publication Legal requirements to publish notices in the Gazette are satisfied if published in electronic form. New laws or regulations are published on the official government website instead of in a printed Gazette, and this is legally acceptable.
10 No Right to Insist on Records Being in Electronic Form Individuals cannot demand that government bodies accept or issue documents or conduct transactions in electronic form. A citizen cannot force a government department to accept an electronic application if they only accept paper forms.
11 Electronic Contracts Contracts formed electronically are legally valid and enforceable. Purchasing goods online and agreeing to terms via a checkbox forms a valid contract between the buyer and the seller.
12 Attribution of Electronic Records An electronic record is considered to be sent by the originator if sent by the originator, an authorized person, or an automated system programmed by the originator. An automated confirmation email sent by a company's system is considered to be sent by the company itself.
13 Acknowledgement of receipt Defines how and when acknowledgment of receipt of electronic communications occurs, and implications if acknowledgment is not received. If a sender specifies that a contract is only binding upon receipt acknowledgment and does not receive it, the contract is not binding.
14 Time and Place of Dispatch and Receipt of Electronic Records Establishes when and where an electronic record is considered sent and received, based on information systems used. An email is considered sent when it leaves the sender's server and received when it enters the recipient's server, determining legal timing of communication.
15 Variation by Agreement Parties can agree to vary certain provisions regarding electronic communications as prescribed by regulations. Two businesses agree on specific protocols for sending and receiving electronic invoices and orders beyond the standard legal provisions.
16 Liability of Certification Service Providers Certification Service Providers are not liable for third-party information they provide access to, under certain conditions. A digital certificate provider is not responsible if a user transmits illegal content using their certification services without the provider's knowledge.
17 Avoidance of Doubt Clarifies that electronic signatures and records are valid for contract formation and that automated systems can form valid contracts. A contract formed when a customer makes a purchase through an online shopping cart is valid, even though no human intervened in the transaction.
